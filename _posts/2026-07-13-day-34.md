what_i_learned: |
  Today, I worked with my research group on developing the script for our elevator pitch. We spent time brainstorming ideas, discussing the most important aspects of 
  our project, and deciding how to communicate our research in a concise and engaging way. Through this process, I learned that creating an effective elevator pitch is 
  not just about summarizing a project—it is about identifying the key message and presenting it in a way that quickly captures the audience’s attention. As a group, we
  discussed which parts of our research should be emphasized and how to explain our work to people who may not have a background in machine learning or healthcare AI. 
  This helped me think more critically about the purpose of our project and the broader impact of investigating fairness in ECG classification models. We also met with 
  the Writing Center to receive feedback on our literature review and introduction sections. During this meeting, we discussed areas where our writing could be 
  improved, including organization, clarity, and how effectively we connected previous research to our own work. Based on the feedback we received, we made revisions 
  to strengthen our paper and improve the overall flow of our argument. This experience taught me the value of outside feedback and how even strong writing can benefit 
  from additional review and refinement.

reflection: |
  Today helped me realize how important communication is in research. Much of our work focuses on coding, data analysis, and machine learning, but none of that 
  matters if we cannot effectively explain what we are doing and why it is important. Working on the elevator pitch challenged me to think about how to present our 
  project in a way that is both clear and interesting. I enjoyed collaborating with my group to develop ideas for the pitch because everyone brought different 
  perspectives to the discussion. Hearing different suggestions helped us refine our message and think more carefully about what makes our project meaningful. It was 
  also interesting to see how difficult it can be to condense months of research into a short presentation. Meeting with the Writing Center was also valuable because 
  it gave us constructive feedback from an outside perspective. Sometimes when you spend a lot of time working on a paper, it becomes difficult to notice areas that 
  need improvement. The feedback helped us identify weaknesses in our literature review and introduction and gave us a clearer direction for revision. Overall, I felt 
  productive today because we made progress on both our presentation skills and our written research, which are both essential parts of the research process.
